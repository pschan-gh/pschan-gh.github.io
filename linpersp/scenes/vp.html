Perspectival Projection

Given an observing "eyeball" $O$, we choose a picture plane $\Pi$ at a fixed distance from $O$.

For any point $P$ in space, on either side of $\Pi$, we define the seat of $P$ to be the unique point $P^{\dagger}$ on $\Pi$ such that the line segment $\overline{P^{\dagger}P}$ is perpendicular to $\Pi$. This point always exists because any line which is not parallel to a plane must intersect that plane at a unique point.

We denote by $Z$ the seat of $O$. We call it the center of $\Pi$.

Let $A$ be any point in space on the other side of the picture plane $\Pi$ from $O$. Its perspectival projection $A'$ is by definiton the unique point where the line passing through $A$ and $O$ intersects $\Pi$. Let $A^{\dagger}$ denote the seat of $A$.

If $\overline{OA}$ is perpendicular to $\Pi$, then then $A' = Z$, since there is exactly one unique line passing through $O$ which is perpendicular to $\Pi$.

Assume now that $\overline{OA}$ is not perpendicular to $\Pi$. We claim that $O, Z, A, A'$, and $A^{\dagger}$ all belong to the same plane. To see this, observe that by assumption the points $O, Z, A$ are non-colinear (i.e. not all on the same line). It is known that any three non-colinear points determine a unique plane containing all three. Let $\mathcal{P}$ be the plane which contains $O, Z, A$. Since $\overline{AA^{\dagger}}$ and $\overline{OZ}$ are both perpendicular to $\Pi$, they are parallel to each other. This implies that $A^{\dagger}$ lies on $\mathcal{P}$.

Moreover, since $\mathcal{P}$ contains the line segment $\overline{OZ}$ which is perpendicular to $\Pi$, the point $\mathcal{P}$ cannot be parallel to $\Pi$. Hence, $\mathcal{P}$ intersects $\Pi$ at a unique line $\mathbf{K}$. Since $Z$ and $A^{\dagger}$ belong to both $\mathcal{P}$ and $\Pi$, they must both lie on $\mathbf{K}$. Since $Z \neq A^{\dagger}$, it follows that $\mathbf{K}$ is the unique line passing through $Z$ and $A^{\dagger}$. Since $A'$ by definition must lie on $\Pi$ and also on $\overline{OA}$, which lies on $\mathcal{P}$, the point $A'$ must belong to the intersection of $\mathcal{P}$ and $\Pi$. Hence, $A'$ lies in the intersection of $\overline{OA}$ and $\mathbf{K}$. Since $\mathbf{K}$ lies on $\Pi$, and $\overline{OA}$ intersects $\Pi$ at exactly one point, the intersection of $\overline{OA}$ and $\mathbf{K}$ consists of $A'$ alone.

In practice, the point $A'$ may be found on the picture plane as follows: First, find the seat $A^{\dagger}$ on $\Pi$. Then, draw the line $\mathbf{K}$ through the points $Z$ and $A^{\dagger}$. Draw a point $O^{\ast}$ on $\Pi$ such that the distance between $O^{\ast}$ and the center $Z$ is equal to that between $O$ and $Z$. Next, draw a point $A^{\ast}$ on $\Pi$, on the other side of $\overline{OA^{\dagger}}$ from $O^{\ast}$, such that the distance between $A^{\dagger}$ and $A^{\ast}$ is equal to that between $A$ and $\Pi$ (or, equivalently, the length of $\overline{A^{\dagger}A})$.

Draw the line segment $\overline{O^{\ast}A^{\ast}}$. Where this line segment intersects $\mathbf{K}$ is the location of $A'$, the perspectival projection of the point $A$.

(Essentially, we are rotating the plane containing $O, Z, A, A'$ and $A^{\dagger}$ about the line $\mathbf{K}$ by 90 degrees.)

In fact, it may be shown that $A'$ always lie within the line segment $\overline{ZA^{\dagger}}$, so in practice one could have drawn $\overline{ZA^{\dagger}}$ instead of $\mathbf{K}$.

Alternatively, one could fix a frame of reference, and a suitably normalized unit of measurement, so that the observer eyeball corresponds to the origin $(0, 0, 0)$ in Cartesian space and the picture plane corresponds to the plane defined by the equation $x = 1$. In such a setup, the perspectival projection of any point in 3-dimensional space with coordinates $(x, y, z)$ is simply $(1, y/x, z/x)$ provided that $x \neq 0$.

In principle, this is all one needs to render images in linear perspective, especially with the help of modern computing technology. Without the aid of such tools, finding the perspectival projection of more complicated objects point-by-point in a purely computational manner could be tedious, and it is useful to develop a set of laws which describe how higher order geometric objects like lines, ellipses and cubes, etc, transform under perspectival projection.

One of the most important of these laws concerns the perspectival projection of straight lines. In other words, viewing a line or line segment as a collection of infinitely many points, what does the set of perspectival projections of all these points look like? Is it a line, part of a line, or something else entirely?

There two types of lines to consider: Transversals and Non-Transversals.

By definition, a transversal line is a line which is parallel to the picture plane. For any transveral line $\mathbf{L}$, its perspectival projection is the intersection of $\Pi$ with the unique plane which contains both $O$ and $\mathbf{L}$. In particular, the projection is a line on $\Pi$ which is parallel to the original line.

For any two points $A$ And $B$ on a transversal line, its follows from Euclidean geometry that the distance between their perspectival projections $A'$ and $B'$ is equal to the distance between $A$ and $B$ divided by a fixed factor $s$ defined as follows: \[ s = 1 + \frac{\text{distance between transversal line and $\Pi$}} {\text{distance between the observer and $\Pi$}} \]

Notice that this factor $s$ is independent of the position of $A$ and $B$ on the transversal line. This implies that there is no distortion in the perspectival projection of a transversal line, in the sense that evenly spaced points on the transversal line (e.g. points on a ruler) project to evenly spaced points on $\Pi$. This is a useful fact to be exploited later in the discussion of measuring points.

Also, if the transversal line happens to lie on the picture plane, then $s = 1$, which makes sense since in this case the line is the same as its own perspectival projection.

The second type of lines we consider are the non-transversal lines, namely lines which are not parallel to the picture plane.

Given any non-transversal line $\mathbf{L}$, we define the directional of $\mathbf{L}$ to be the unique line $\mathbf{D}$ passing through $O$ which is parallel to $\mathbf{L}$. (Brook Taylor called $\mathbf{D}$ the parallel of the original line $\mathbf{L}$.)

Since $\mathbf{L}$ is by assumption not transversal, neither is $\mathbf{D}$. So, $\mathbf{D}$ intersects the picture plane at a unique point $V$. We call this point $V$ the vanishing point of $\mathbf{L}$.

Theorem. Let $B$ be the point where $\mathbf{L}$ intersects the picture plane. The perspectival projection of every point on $\mathbf{L}$ (on the other side of $\Pi$ from $O$) must lie on the line segment $\overline{VB}$, and vice versa: every point on the line segment $\overline{VB}$, except possibly $V$ itself, is a perspectival projection of a point on $\mathbf{L}$.
Proof.

Let $A$ be any point on $\mathbf{L}$ on the other side of the picture plane from $O$. If $\mathbf{L}$ passes through $O$, then $B = V$, and the "line segment" $\overline{VB}$ is just the point $V$.

From now on, suppose $\mathbf{L}$ does not pass through $O$. Linear algebra tells us that there is a unique plane $\mathcal{P}$ which contains both the point $O$ and the line $\mathbf{L}$. Moreover, since $\mathbf{L}$ is non-transversal, $\mathcal{P}$ is not parallel to $\Pi$, so the the intersection of these two planes is a line $\mathbf{L}'$. By construction, the line segment $\overline{VB}$ contains both the point $V$ and the point $B$. Since $V$ and $B$ both lie on $\Pi$ and $\mathcal{P}$, they both lie on $\mathbf{L}'$. Hence, $\overline{VB}$ is part of $\mathbf{L}'$.

By definition, the perspectival projection $A'$ of $A$ must lie on the line segment $\overline{OA}$, which in turn lies on $\mathcal{P}$. Moreover, since $A'$ is the unique point where $\overline{OA}$ intersects $\Pi$, it must belong to $\mathbf{L}'$, the intersection of these two planes.

It remains to show why $A'$ lies on the line segment $\overline{VB}$.

Since $A'$ lies on the line segment $\overline{OA}$, we have: \[ \overrightarrow{A'A} = \alpha \overrightarrow{A'O}\\ \] for some number $\alpha \lt 0$.

Since $\mathbf{L}$ is parallel to $\overline{OV}$, \[ \overrightarrow{BA} = \beta \overrightarrow{VO}, \] for some $\beta \lt 0$.

Likewise, \[ \overrightarrow{A'B} = \gamma \overrightarrow{A'V} \] for some number $\gamma$. The sign of $\gamma$ will tell us whether $A'$ lies "between" $V$ and $B$.

Since: \begin{align*} \overrightarrow{A'A} &= \overrightarrow{A'B} + \overrightarrow{BA}\\ \overrightarrow{A'O} &= \overrightarrow{A'V} + \overrightarrow{VO'},\\ \end{align*} we have: \begin{align*} \overrightarrow{A'B} &=\overrightarrow{A'A} - \overrightarrow{BA}\\ \overrightarrow{A'V} &=\overrightarrow{A'O} - \overrightarrow{VO}\\ \gamma\overrightarrow{A'V} &=\alpha\overrightarrow{A'O} - \beta\overrightarrow{VO}\\ \end{align*}

It follows that: \[ \vec{0} = (\gamma - \alpha)\overrightarrow{A'O} - (\gamma - \beta)\overrightarrow{VO}\\ \] Since $\overrightarrow{A'O}$ and $\overrightarrow{VO}$ are not parallel (hence linearly independent), we have must have: \[ \gamma - \alpha = \gamma - \beta = 0. \] So, $\gamma = \alpha \lt 0$, implying that the vectors $\ora{A'V}$ and $\ora{A'B}$ point in opposite directions. Hence, it follows that $A'$ lies on the line segment $\overline{VB}$ between the points $V$ and $B$.

Conversely, let $A'$ be any point strictly between $V$ and $B$ on $\overline{VB}$. We want to show that $A'$ is the perspectival projection of a point $A$ on $\mathbf{L}$.

Let $\mathbf{M}$ be the line passing through $O$ and $A'$. Since both $O$ and $A$ lies on $\mathcal{P}$. The line $\mathbf{M}$ must also lie on $\mathcal{P}$. Moreover, since $A'$ is not equal to $V$, the line $\mathbf{M}$ is non-parallel to $\mathbf{D}$, and hence also non-parallel to $\mathbf{L}$. By school geometry applied to the plane $\mathcal{P}$, the non-parallel lines $\mathbf{M}$ and $\mathbf{L}$ must intersect at a unqiue point $A$.

It remains to show that $A$ is on the other side of $\Pi$ from $O$. We have:

\[ \ora{BA} = \ora{A'A} - \ora{A'B} \] \[ \ora{VO} = \ora{A'O} - \ora{A'V} \] Since $\ora{BA} \parallel \ora{VO}$, there exists some number $\gamma$ such that: \[ \ora{BA} = \gamma\ora{VO}. \] Likewise, there are numbers $\alpha, \beta$ such that: \[ \ora{A'A} = \alpha\ora{A'O}, \quad \ora{A'B} = \beta\ora{A'V}. \] Moreover, since by assumption $A'$ is strictly between $V$ and $B$, we know that $\beta \lt 0$.

Hence, we have: \begin{align*} \gamma\ora{VO} &= \alpha\ora{A'O} - \beta\ora{A'V}\\ \ora{VO} &= \ora{A'O} - \ora{A'V}, \end{align*} which implies that: \[ (\gamma - \beta)\ora{VO} = - (\alpha - \beta)\ora{A'O}. \] Since $\ora{VO} \nparallel \ora{VO}$, both $\gamma - \beta$ and $\alpha - \beta$ must be equal to $0$. In particular, $\gamma = \beta \lt 0,$ which implies that $A$ is on the opposite side of the picture plane from $O$. We conclude that $A'$ is the perspectival projection of a point $A$ on $\mathbf{L}$.

Finding the Vanishing Point on the Picture Plane

If the line $\mathbf{L}$ is orthogonal to the picture plane, then its directional $\mathbf{D}$ is also orthogonal to $\Pi$. The vanishing point $V$ in this case coincides with the so-called center point $Z$ of $\Pi$,

Before we continue with finding the vanishing point for any non-transversal line $\mathbf{L}$. It is useful to first establish the following results:

Theorem. Let $\mathcal{Q}$ be any plane in space which is not parallel to the picture plane. Then, there exists a line $\mathbf{H}$ on $\Pi$ such that the perspectival projection of $\mathcal{Q}$ terminates at $\mathbf{H}$, in the sense that the vanishing point of any nontransversal line $\mathbf{N}$ parallel to $\mathcal{Q}$ must lie on $\mathbf{H}$.
Proof.
Let $\mathcal{H}$ be the unique plane containing $O$ which is parallel to $\mathcal{Q}$. Let $\mathbf{H}$ be the intersection of $\mathcal{H}$ and the picture plane. Given any line $\mathbf{J}$ which is parallel to $\mathcal{Q}$, its directional $\mathbf{D}$ passes through $O$ and is parallel to $\mathbf{H}$. Hence, $\mathbf{D}$ lies on $\mathcal{H}$. By definition, the vanishing point of $\mathbf{J}$ is the point where $\mathbf{D}$ intersects $\Pi$. Since by construction the line $\mathbf{H}$ is the intersection of $\mathcal{H}$ and $\Pi$, it follows that the vanishing point of $\mathbf{J}$ lies on $\mathbf{H}$.

Also, by construction,

Corollary. The line $\mathbf{H}$ is parallel to the intersection of $\mathcal{Q}$ with $\Pi$.

We call this line $\mathbf{H}$ the vanishing line of the plane $\mathcal{Q}$. It may be shown that any two planes which are parallel to each other, but not parallel to $\Pi$, have the same vanishing line.

In the common situation where the observer has fixed a frame of reference giving meaning to such qualities as "vertical" and "horizontal" (notice that these terms have never been used so far in our discussion), the vanishing line of any horizontal plane is precisely the horizon discussed in most introductory texts on linear perspective.

Now, back to the non-transveral line $\mathbf{L}$. Let $V$ be the vanishing point of $\mathbf{L}$. Let $B$ be the point where $\mathbf{L}$ intersects $\Pi$. Let $\mathbf{K}$ be the line orthogonal to $\Pi$ which intersects $\mathbf{L}$ at the point $B$. (In fact, any other point on $\mathbf{L}$ will work just fine. We just need to fix a point for our demonstration.) Let $\mathcal{G}$ be the unique plane containing $B$ which is parallel to both $\mathbf{K}$ and $\mathbf{L}$. Let's call $\mathcal{G}$ the ground plane of $\mathbf{L}$.

(Note that if $\mathbf{L}$ is orthogonal to $\Pi$, then $\mathbf{L}$ is equal to $\mathbf{K}$. In this case, there are infinitely many planes containing $\mathbf{L}$ which are orthogonal to the $\Pi$.)

Let $\mathbf{H}$ be the vanishing line of $\mathcal{G}$. Let $\mathbf{G}$ be the line which is the intersection of $\mathcal{G}$ and $\Pi$. Then, by the theorems above, the line $\mathbf{H}$ is parallel to $\mathbf{G}$. Moreover, both the center point $Z$ and the vanishing point $V$ lie on $\mathbf{H}$, since they are the vanishing points of lines, $\mathbf{K}$ and $\mathbf{L}$, respectively, which are parallel to $\mathcal{G}$.

We call the lines $\mathbf{H}$ and $\mathbf{G}$ the horizon and ground line, respectively, associated with $\mathbf{L}$.

It may be shown that every point on $\mathbf{G}$ is the seat of a point on $\mathbf{L}$. Hence, we call $\mathbf{G}$ the seat of $\mathbf{L}$. To find $\mathbf{H}$ in practice, we first find the seat $\mathbf{G}$ of $\mathbf{L}$ on $\Pi$, then translate $\mathbf{G}$ without altering its direction so that it passes through $Z$. This shifted $\mathbf{G}$ is equal to $\mathbf{H}$.

Let $\delta$ be the ($\leq 90^\circ$) angle between $\mathbf{K}$ and $\mathbf{L}$. Then, the angle between the line segments $\overline{OZ}$ and $\overline{OV}$ is also $\delta$. To find the location of $V$ on the horizon $\mathbf{H}$, imagine rotating the triangle $\Delta ZOV$ about $\mathbf{H}$ by 90 degrees. Then, $O$ is transformed to a point $\hat{O}$ on $\Pi$ whose distance to $Z$ is equal to that between $O$ and $Z$, and the angle between $\overline{\hat{O}Z}$ and $\overline{\hat{O}V}$ is also equal to $\delta$.

Hence, once the horizon $\mathbf{H}$ associated with $\mathbf{L}$ has been determined, one can find the location of $V$ this way:

  • First, draw a point $\hat{O}$ on $\Pi$ such that $\overline{\hat{O}Z}$ is perpendicular to $\mathbf{H}$, and the distance between $\hat{O}$ and $Z$ is equal to that between $O$ and $Z$.
  • Then, draw a line through $\hat{O}$ such that the angle between the line and $\overline{\hat{O}Z}$ is $\delta$. Where this line intersects $\mathbf{H}$ is the vanishing point $V$ of $\mathbf{L}$.
