Let $\mathbf{L}$ be an non-transversal line, and $\mathbf{L}'$ its perspectival projection on the picture plane.

(From henceforth, the perspectival projection of any object will be labelled by affixing the $\prime$ symbol to the label of the original object.)

Let $\mathbf{J}$ (the "ruler") be any transversal line passing through point $A$ which is not parallel to $\mathbf{L}'$.

Let $B$ be any point on $\mathbf{J}$. Its perspectival projection on the picture plane is the point $B'$ on $\mathbf{J}'$ such that: \[ \frac{\left|\overline{A'B'}\right|}{\left|\overline{AB}\right|} = \frac{\left|\overline{OA'}\right|}{\left|\overline{OA}\right|} \]

Let $C$ be a point on the line $\mathbf{L}$ which has the same distance to $A$ as $B$. This document addresses the problem of finding $C'$.

We define the directional of $\mathbf{L}$ to be the line through $O$ which is parallel to $\mathbf{L}$. The vanishing point $V$ of $\mathbf{L}$ is the point where the directional of $\mathbf{L}$ intersects the picture plane.

We take the measuring line $\mathbf{K}$ to be the unique line passing through $V$ which is parallel to $\mathbf{J}$.

With regard to finding $C'$, there are two cases to consider:

Case 1: The point $C$ is closer to the picture plane than the base point $A$.

In this case, we take $M$ to be the point on $\mathbf{K}$ of distance $\left|\overline{OV}\right|$ to $V$, which lies on the "same side" of $\mathbf{L}'$ as $B'$.

Then, the triangle $\Delta VMO$ is an isoceles triangle, just like $\Delta ABC$. Moreover, by construction: \[ \overline{VM} \parallel \overline{AB} \quad \text{ and } \quad \overline{VO} \parallel \overline{AC} \] Hence, from school geometry the triangle $\Delta ABC$ is similar to $\Delta VMO$. In fact, stronger than similar: each pair of corresponding sides of the two triangles are parallel.

In particular: \[ \overline{OM} \parallel \overline{CB} \] which implies that the point $M$ coincides with the vanishing point of $\overline{CB}$.

Case 2: The point $C$ is further from the picture plane than the base point $A$.

In this case, let $\tilde{C}$ be the point on the directional of $\mathbf{L}$ which is on the other side of the picture plane from $O$, such that $\left|\overline{OC}\right| = \left|\overline{OV}\right|$.

By essentially the same reasoning used in Case 1, the triangle $\Delta V\tilde{B}\tilde{C}$ is similar to $\Delta ABC$, with parallel corresponding sides.

Take $M$ to be the reflection of $\tilde{B}$ over the point $V$. Then, $\Delta VMO$ is the reflection of $\Delta V\tilde{B}\tilde{C}$ through $V$, and hence: \[ \overline{OM} \parallel \overline{BC}. \] This implies that $M$ is the vanishing point of the line segment $\overline{BC}$.

In practical terms, $M$ is the point on $\mathbf{K}$ of distance $\left|\overline{OV}\right|$ to $V$, which lies on the "opposite side" of $\mathbf{L}'$ as $B'$.

We call the point $M$ the measuring point of the line $\mathbf{L}$.

In either case,
since $C$ lies on the line segment $\overline{BC}$, its perspectival projection $C'$ lies on the line passing through $B'$ and $M$. On the other hand, since $C$ also lies on $\mathbf{L}$, the point $C'$ must also lie on $\mathbf{L}'$. So, $C'$ must be the unique intersection of the line segment $\overline{B'M}$ with $\mathbf{L}'$.

Remark:
Note that the ruler $\mathbf{J}'$ can be taken to be any line different from $\mathbf{L}'$ passing through the base point $A$, as long as the condition $\mathbf{J}' \parallel \mathbf{K}$ is maintained.
